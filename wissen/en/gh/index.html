General Hardness (GH) in the Aquarium

General hardness tells you how much calcium and magnesium is dissolved in the water — essential for osmoregulation, shrimp molting, and plant nutrition. Here's the ideal range and how GH differs from KH.

What general hardness actually shows

General hardness (GH) mainly measures the concentration of calcium and magnesium ions in the water. Both minerals are essential for fish osmoregulation (their internal salt-water balance) and, for shrimp and snails, additionally crucial for building shell or exoskeleton.

GH and KH are often confused, but they measure different things: KH measures buffering capacity (bicarbonate), GH measures total calcium and magnesium content. Water can absolutely have high GH with low KH or vice versa — it's worth checking both separately.

Why GH fluctuates

The most common causes of change:

01

Evaporation: When water evaporates, minerals stay behind — GH creeps upward if you only top off with tap water instead of RO water.

02

Changing tap water: Your utility's GH can shift slightly by season.

03

Mineral-based decor: Limestone rocks or crushed coral continuously release minerals and slowly raise GH.

04

Remineralizing products: Deliberately dosed GH boosters for shrimp or hard-water fish raise the value on purpose.

What to do about deviations

If GH is too low: dedicated GH-boosting products (calcium/magnesium mineral salts) added directly to the water, dosed per the manufacturer's instructions. Especially important for shrimp, since too-low GH can cause molting problems.

If GH is too high: mixing in RO or distilled water during water changes lowers GH gradually. When topping off evaporated water, use only RO water instead of tap water to avoid a slow creep upward.

Important for shrimp keepers: Low GH is one of the most common yet least obvious causes of molting problems and sudden shrimp deaths — when losses are unexplained, this is always worth checking.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between GH and KH?
GH measures calcium and magnesium content (mineral hardness), KH measures buffering capacity via bicarbonate. The two are independent of each other.
What GH value is ideal?
6 to 12 °dGH is the standard range for most community tanks.
Why does GH matter especially for shrimp?
Too-low GH makes molting harder for shrimp and can lead to molting problems or death.
