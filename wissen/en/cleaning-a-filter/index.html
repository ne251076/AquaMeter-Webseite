Cleaning a Filter: How to Do It Right

Your aquarium filter is the heart of biological filtration — which is exactly why cleaning it incorrectly is one of the most common mistakes beginners make without realizing it. Here's the right approach.

Why filter cleaning is so delicate

Inside the filter live not just mechanical filter media, but above all nitrifying bacteria that break down ammonia through nitrite into nitrate. This bacterial colony is the real "engine" of biological filtration — and it's sensitive to overly thorough or overly frequent cleaning.

Treating the filter like a kitchen sink and scrubbing everything under hot tap water kills off a large portion of this bacterial colony. The result: a small "mini-cycle" effect with a renewed ammonia or nitrite spike, even though the tank had long since been fully cycled.

How often should you clean it?

As a rule of thumb: every 4 to 6 weeks, depending on stocking density and filter capacity. Heavily stocked tanks or filters with noticeably reduced flow need cleaning sooner; lightly stocked planted tanks often need it far less often.

Key principle: Never clean or replace all filter media at the same time. Always change only a portion (e.g. half of the biological media), so enough bacterial colony survives to quickly repopulate the rest.

The steps, in the right order

01

Unplug the filter

Before doing any work on the filter, turn it off and unplug it from power.

02

Scoop out some tank water into a bucket

This water will be used to rinse the filter media shortly — never use tap water for this.

03

Open the filter and remove media

Take out only part of the biological media (e.g. ceramic rings, filter floss) — leave the rest inside the filter.

04

Rinse in the removed tank water

Gently swish the media in the bucket of tank water until coarse debris is removed. Don't wring or scrub it — the bacteria sit on the surface and get scraped off that way.

05

Replace mechanical filter floss if needed

Pure mechanical floss pads (which only catch coarse particles and don't carry much bacterial colony) can be fully replaced if needed.

06

Reassemble and reconnect the filter

Put the media back in their original order, close the filter, plug it back in, and briefly check that it's running properly.

Common mistakes

✕

Rinsing with tap water: The chlorine it contains reliably kills off the bacterial colony.

✕

Replacing all media at once: Removes too much bacterial colony at once, triggering a renewed ammonia/nitrite spike.

✕

Cleaning too often "just to be safe": Disturbs the bacterial colony unnecessarily often — less frequent is usually better here.

✕

Using dish soap or disinfectants: Residue is toxic to fish and destroys the bacterial colony completely.

After cleaning: watch closely for a bit

After cleaning a filter, it's worth monitoring ammonia and nitrite a bit more closely for a few days — especially if you weren't sure how much you cleaned. A brief, minimal rise is normal but should settle back down within a few days.
