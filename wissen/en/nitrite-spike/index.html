The Nitrite Spike: Why It Happens and What To Do

Right when you start feeling safe after surviving the ammonia phase, the nitrite spike often hits. Here's why it happens and how to respond correctly.

Typical cycling timeline
Nitrite Spike
Day 1 Week 2 Week 4 Week 6

What exactly is the nitrite spike?

The nitrite spike is the second critical phase in a new aquarium's nitrogen cycle. First, bacteria that break down ammonia into nitrite establish themselves. The bacteria that convert nitrite further into far less harmful nitrate, however, establish noticeably more slowly.

The result: while the ammonia reading is already dropping, the nitrite reading often keeps rising — typically in week 2 to 4 after setup. Right at this moment, many beginners wrongly start feeling safe, since "the ammonia test was already green."

How do you recognize a nitrite spike?

A water test shows a measurable nitrite reading, often rising over several days. With fish already stocked, visible symptoms can also appear: noticeably pale to brownish gills, fish gasping for air despite plenty of oxygen in the water, and general lethargy.

Important: These symptoms occur because nitrite blocks the blood's ability to carry oxygen ("brown blood disease") — not from actual oxygen deficiency in the water itself. More in the Nitrite article.

What to do during a nitrite spike

01

Immediate water change

Change 30 to 50% of the water to dilute the concentration directly.

02

Aquarium salt as an emergency measure

Chloride ions from plain, additive-free salt briefly block nitrite uptake through the gills — this doesn't replace a water change though, just a stopgap.

03

Pause feeding

Skip feeding for 1 to 2 days to avoid adding further load to the bacterial population.

04

Add extra aeration

An air stone raises oxygen levels and eases the suffocating effect of nitrite.

05

Keep testing patiently, daily

A nitrite spike typically takes 1 to 2 weeks to resolve on its own, once the right bacterial colony has established itself.

Can you prevent a nitrite spike?

You can rarely avoid it entirely, but you can noticeably soften it: starter bacteria added during setup noticeably speed up the establishment of nitrite-consuming bacteria. A slow, gradual approach to stocking — instead of adding all fish at once — also reduces the load the bacterial colony has to cope with.

Most important, though, remains: only add fish once ammonia and nitrite have held steady at 0 for several days. More on the full cycling phase in our Setting Up an Aquarium guide and the interactive Cycling Checklist.
