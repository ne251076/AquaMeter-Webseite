Iron in the Aquarium

Iron (Fe) is largely irrelevant for fish, but essential for healthy plant growth — especially for red and fast-growing species. Here's a sensible range and how to dose correctly.

Typical ideal range
0.1 – 0.3ppm
0.1–0.3 ppm ideal (planted tank) < 0.1 ppm possible deficiency > 0.5 ppm risky for shrimp

Why iron only matters for planted tanks

Iron is one of the most important micronutrients for aquarium plants — it's needed for chlorophyll production and is especially critical for red plant species, whose signature color depends directly on iron availability. Iron plays almost no role for fish, which is why this reading only matters at all in planted tanks.

Worth knowing: the iron measured in the water is usually just the short-term leftover after your most recent fertilizing — plants take it up within hours to days. A test right after dosing will look very different from one right before.

Signs of iron deficiency

The classic clue is chlorosis: new leaves grow in noticeably pale or yellowish while older leaves stay normal green (since plants can't redistribute iron from old leaves to new ones). Red plant species also often lose color intensity and look washed out.

Why the reading fluctuates

01

Fertilizing schedule: Without regular iron dosing, the reading drops to near zero quickly since plants keep consuming it.

02

pH level: At higher pH, non-chelated iron becomes insoluble faster and less available to plants — chelated iron fertilizers have an advantage here.

03

Plant growth rate: Fast-growing or strongly red-colored plants consume noticeably more iron than slow-growing species.

What to do about deviations

For deficiency signs: fertilize regularly in small amounts ("little and often") instead of occasional large doses — this matches how plants actually take up nutrients and avoids swings. At higher pH, use chelated iron fertilizers (e.g. with EDTA, DTPA, or gluconate), since these stay plant-available longer even at elevated pH.

Important for shrimp keepers: Shrimp are more sensitive to excess iron than most fish. In shrimp tanks, dose more cautiously and at lower levels than in a pure planted tank without invertebrates.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do fish need iron?
No, iron is only relevant for plants and plays no direct role for fish.
How do I recognize iron deficiency in plants?
The classic sign is chlorosis — yellowish new leaves while older leaves stay green.
How often should I fertilize with iron?
Regularly in small amounts rather than occasionally in large doses — that matches how plants actually consume it.
