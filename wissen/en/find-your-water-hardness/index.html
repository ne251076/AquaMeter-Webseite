Find Your Real Water Hardness

Before you adjust GH and KH with remineralizers or RO water, it helps to know what's actually coming out of your tap. Here are the most reliable free sources — instead of estimated numbers.

Why we don't offer our own lookup database here

Honestly: there's no single, publicly accessible, up-to-date database that accurately covers every address worldwide — water hardness is set by thousands of individual utilities and can even vary within the same city depending on which treatment plant serves your street. Rather than showing you estimated or outdated numbers, we'd rather point you to the sources that actually know for certain.

The most reliable sources

Your local water utility

The most accurate source there is. In the US, utilities are legally required to publish an annual Consumer Confidence Report (CCR) with hardness and mineral content — search "[your city] water quality report" or "[your utility] CCR". In the UK, water companies publish hardness by postcode on their websites.

EPA / national drinking water databases

In the US, the EPA and organizations like the Environmental Working Group maintain searchable tap water quality databases by utility or ZIP code.

Test strips

A cheap, direct way to measure hardness yourself if you can't easily find an official figure — GH/KH test strips or drop tests work fine for tap water, not just aquarium water.

For comparison: most freshwater community tanks do well around 6–12 °dGH (roughly 107–214 ppm) — more in our GH article.

What to do once you know your number

Once you know your actual tap water hardness, you can make targeted decisions: for very hard tap water and soft-water-loving species, diluting with RO water is worth it. For very soft tap water and hard-water-loving species (e.g. Malawi cichlids), targeted remineralizing helps. Still test directly in your tank regularly — official tap water figures don't account for changes caused by your substrate or decor.

Practical tip: Once you know your number, run it through our Water Parameter Advisor to see whether it fits a typical community tank.
