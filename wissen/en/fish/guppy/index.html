Guppy (Poecilia reticulata)

The guppy is one of the most popular beginner fish — colorful, easy to care for, and remarkably prolific. Here are the key facts for proper care.

OriginNorthern South America, Caribbean
Sizeup to 2.4 in
Tank sizemin. 14 gal, better 16 gal or more
Social structuregroup with more females than males (e.g. 1:2)
Temperature75–79°F
pH level7.0–8.5
General hardness (GH)10–20 °dGH
Carbonate hardness (KH)5–20 °dKH
Lifespan2–3 years
DietOmnivore: flake food, live/frozen food, algae

Water parameters in detail

Guppies prefer hard, alkaline water — unlike other popular beginner fish such as the neon tetra, which needs soft, acidic water. Keep this in mind if you're thinking of combining both species (see tank mates below). More on the individual readings in our GH and KH articles.

Important: Driftwood in the tank slightly acidifies the water — not ideal for a dedicated guppy tank if you want to keep that high pH stable.

Tank mates

Good fit

  • Platy and molly (similar water needs)
  • Corydoras catfish
  • Otocinclus

Not suitable

  • Fin nippers
  • Larger predators like angelfish or male bettas

Notable traits

Guppies are livebearers — females give birth to free-swimming fry directly, no eggs involved. Since there's no parental care, adults often eat their own young. Dense planting (e.g. java moss) gives fry places to hide and noticeably improves survival rates.

Guppies reproduce very quickly — a female can produce several batches of fry from a single mating, since sperm is stored internally. Factor that into your stocking plans.

Common mistakes

The most common beginner mistakes: too small a tank, housing them with predatory fish, skipping slow acclimation when adding new fish (temperature/water shock), and water that's too soft for their natural needs.
