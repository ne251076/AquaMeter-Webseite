Dwarf Cichlid (Apistogramma spec.)

Apistogramma dwarf cichlids captivate with intense colors and fascinating breeding behavior. The robust cockatoo dwarf cichlid makes a particularly good starting point.

OriginSouth America, mostly Peru / upper Amazon basin
Sizemales ~2.8–3.1 in, females smaller (~2 in)
Tank sizemin. 24 in footprint for 1 male + 2 females
Social structure1 male with 2+ females (harem) — never multiple males together
Temperature75–82°F
pH level6.0–7.5
General hardness (GH)4–12 °dGH
Carbonate hardness (KH)0–8 °dKH
Lifespan3–5 years
DietPrefers carnivore diet: live/frozen food, supplemented with dry food

Most important rule: only one male per tank

Never keep multiple males of the same species together. It almost always leads to fierce, sometimes fatal territorial fights — even in large tanks. One male with several females (a harem) is the only recommended setup.

Water parameters in detail

The cockatoo dwarf cichlid (Apistogramma cacatuoides) is considered one of the more robust and easier-to-keep species in the genus — compared to other Apistogramma species, it also tolerates somewhat harder, more alkaline water. Other species in the genus often need noticeably softer, more acidic water.

Tank mates

Good fit

  • Small, peaceful tetras
  • Corydoras catfish
  • Rasboras

Not suitable

  • Very active species (e.g. swordtails)
  • Shrimp (often seen as food)
  • Other large cichlids

Notable traits

Dwarf cichlids need plenty of hiding spots and visual barriers — coconut shells, clay pots, and dense edge planting break up the tank visually and help avoid conflict. Females in a harem also need their own retreat spots to escape aggression from other females or the male.

Common mistakes

The most common beginner mistakes: multiple males in the same tank, too little structure and hiding spots, and housing them with shrimp, which easily become prey.
