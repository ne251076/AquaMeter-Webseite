Dropsy

Dropsy is one of the most serious conditions that can occur in an aquarium — here are the key facts, presented honestly.

CauseUsually bacterial, less often viral or parasitic
AffectsInternal organs (liver, kidneys)
Contagion riskHigh — isolate immediately
PrognosisOften poor, since usually caught late

Important to know: a symptom, not a standalone disease

Strictly speaking, dropsy isn't a disease name but a description of a symptom — a swollen belly caused by fluid buildup. The actual underlying cause can be a bacterial infection, less commonly viruses, intestinal parasites, or disrupted osmoregulation.

Recognizing symptoms

Characteristic signs are an increasingly bloated belly and scales that stick out (giving the fish a pinecone-like appearance) — both signs, however, usually only become clearly visible in the late, advanced stage. Other possible signs: bulging eyes ("pop-eye"), inflammation around the vent, lethargy, and reduced flight response.

Why the prognosis is often poor

The bloated belly is usually a late warning sign — by this point, internal organs like the liver and kidneys are often already severely damaged. That explains why recovery chances are low in many cases, even with immediate treatment.

What you should do

01

Isolate immediately

Sick fish shed the responsible bacteria into the water in large quantities — a separate tank protects the remaining fish.

02

Check and optimize water parameters

Check nitrite and ammonia readings, do a water change if needed — poor water quality is one of the main triggers.

03

Consult a veterinarian

A vet experienced with ornamental fish can narrow down the cause more precisely and treat it accordingly — especially worthwhile for valuable or especially loved fish.

Being honest: in advanced, hopeless cases of dropsy, the animal's welfare should come first. Talk to a veterinarian about the options in this situation, rather than letting the fish suffer unnecessarily.
