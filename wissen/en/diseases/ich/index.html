Ich (White Spot Disease)

Ich, short for Ichthyophthirius, is the most common disease in aquariums by far — fortunately also very treatable if you respond in time.

PathogenIchthyophthirius multifiliis (ciliate protozoan)
AffectsSkin and gills
Contagious to humans?No
Typical triggerStress, e.g. from transport or new fish
Treatment duration7–14 days
PrognosisGood with timely treatment

Recognizing symptoms

The clearest sign is small, white spots (roughly 0.02–0.06 in) that first appear on the fins and spread across the body over time. Affected fish often scrape themselves against rocks or decor to relieve itching.

Why does the disease break out right now?

The pathogen is present latently in almost every aquarium without causing an outbreak — similar to how some cold viruses work in humans. Only once the fish's immune system is weakened, for example by the stress of transport, new water conditions, or poor water quality, can the parasite spread.

Why treatment takes several days

Important to understand: the parasite goes through several stages. While it's embedded in the fish's skin, it's protected from medication. Only once it detaches and becomes a free-swimming "tomite" heading toward a new host can it be treated. That's why treatment must continue for several days (usually 7–14) — even after the spots have disappeared.

Treatment

01

Raise the temperature carefully

Raise to around 82–86°F (depending on species) — this speeds up the parasite's life cycle. Important: add extra aeration, since warmer water holds less oxygen.

02

Salt treatment (optional, for salt-tolerant stocking)

Add gradually, following product guidance for your water hardness — split into several small additions over 24 hours to protect filter bacteria.

03

Use a dedicated medication

Commercial ich treatments from a pet store, dosed per the label. Invertebrates like shrimp often can't tolerate many of these treatments — check first.

04

Follow through with the full treatment

Keep treating even after visible spots disappear, since free-swimming stages may still be active in the water.
