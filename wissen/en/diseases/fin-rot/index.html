Fin Rot

Ragged, deteriorating fin edges usually point to fin rot — a bacterial condition that's almost always linked to poor water quality.

PathogenUsually bacteria of the genus Aeromonas or Pseudomonas
AffectsFins (related mouth rot affects the mouth area)
Typical triggerOverstocking, overfeeding, infrequent water changes
Treatment durationA few days to 2 weeks
PrognosisVery good with early treatment

Recognizing symptoms

Early on, only small tears or a slightly frayed edge on the fins may be visible. Left untreated, entire fin sections can rot away over time. The related mouth rot shows the same pattern on the mouth area, which lightens and frays.

Why fin rot is almost always a water-quality issue

The responsible bacteria are present in practically every aquarium — they only become a problem once fish are weakened by poor conditions. The most common triggers: overstocking, overfeeding, and infrequent water changes, which can lead to elevated ammonia or nitrite readings.

Treatment

In the early stage, consistent water hygiene is often enough: a generous water change, thorough tank cleaning, and if needed a gentle filter cleaning to lower bacterial load.

For more advanced cases, antibacterial medication from a pet store is also needed — check the product label and, if in doubt, consult experienced fishkeepers or an aquatic veterinarian.
