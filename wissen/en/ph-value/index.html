pH Level in the Aquarium

The pH level measures how acidic or alkaline your water is – and directly affects whether fish, plants, and bacteria feel at home. Here's the ideal range, why it fluctuates, and what to do about deviations.

What the pH level actually shows

The pH scale runs from 0 to 14: values below 7 are acidic, values above 7 are alkaline, and 7 itself is neutral. In an aquarium it affects almost everything — from how relaxed your fish are to how effectively the nitrifying bacteria in your filter work.

Important to know: there's no single "correct" pH for every tank. What's ideal depends heavily on your stocking — a discus tank needs a different range than a Lake Malawi setup.

Why the pH fluctuates

The most common causes of change:

01

Low carbonate hardness (KH): KH buffers the pH. If it runs too low, the pH can suddenly crash (a so-called "pH crash").

02

CO₂ injection: Added CO₂ lowers the pH during the day, and it rises again at night — a normal fluctuation in planted tanks.

03

Detritus buildup: Organic material breaks down and produces acids that lower the pH over time.

04

Water changes with different tap water: When your tap water has a noticeably different pH than your tank water.

What to do about deviations

If pH is too low: check KH first — that's often the real cause. Raising KH (e.g. with mineral supplements or crushed coral in the filter) usually stabilizes the pH again too.

If pH is too high: frequent, smaller water changes often help already. Peat or dedicated pH-lowering products are options, but should be dosed carefully and gradually.

Important: Never change pH abruptly. A swing of more than 0.3 pH within a few hours is far more dangerous for most fish than a permanently slightly "wrong" but stable value.

Frequently Asked Questions

What pH level is ideal for a community aquarium?
For most community tanks, the ideal range is 6.5 to 7.5. Specific stocking like discus or Malawi cichlids requires different ranges.
Why does my pH level keep fluctuating?
The most common cause is carbonate hardness (KH) that's too low to properly buffer the pH. CO2 swings and detritus breakdown can also affect it.
Is a sudden pH crash dangerous?
Yes, a swing of more than 0.3 pH within a few hours is more dangerous for most fish than a permanently slightly off, but stable, value.
