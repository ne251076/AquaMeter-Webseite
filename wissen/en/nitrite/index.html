Nitrite in the Aquarium

Nitrite (NO2⁻) is the intermediate stage between ammonia and nitrate — and the reason many tanks hit a second, invisible crisis point around week 2 to 4. Here's how to spot it and what to do in an emergency.

Target value
0ppm
0 ppm ideal — any measurable reading is a red flag 0.25–0.5 ppm already critical >0.5 ppm acute danger

Why nitrite is so tricky

Nitrite forms when Nitrosomonas bacteria break down ammonia — the first step in the nitrogen cycle. The real problem: the second step, converting nitrite into far-less-toxic nitrate via Nitrobacter or Nitrospira bacteria, establishes noticeably slower than the first step. This creates a second wave of trouble in new tanks after the ammonia reading has already dropped — many fish keepers wrongly feel safe at exactly that point.

Nitrite is toxic to fish because it binds to hemoglobin in the blood and blocks its ability to carry oxygen (so-called "brown blood disease"). Fish essentially suffocate from the inside even when there's plenty of oxygen in the water. Often noticeable through unusually pale to brownish gills and fish gasping for air.

Why the reading rises

The most common causes, mostly identical to ammonia:

01

Second phase of cycling: The classic "nitrite spike" around week 2–4, when Nitrobacter bacteria haven't yet caught up with the already-established ammonia breakdown.

02

Stocking too fast: New fish added too early or in too large a batch, before the bacterial population has adjusted.

03

Overfeeding: Extra organic load indirectly increases nitrite production too.

04

Filter cleaning with tap water: Chlorine in tap water kills the especially sensitive Nitrobacter bacteria easily.

What to do about elevated readings

01

Immediate water change: Change 30–50% of the water to dilute the concentration.

02

Add aquarium salt: Chloride ions (e.g. from plain, additive-free salt) block nitrite uptake through the gills and work as a fast emergency measure — but don't replace a water change.

03

Stop feeding: Pause for 1–2 days to avoid increasing the load further.

04

Add extra aeration: An air stone raises oxygen levels and eases the suffocating effect of nitrite.

Important: Adding salt against nitrite is a recognized emergency measure, but not a permanent state — once the reading stabilizes, dilute the salt back out gradually through normal water changes instead of salting indefinitely.
