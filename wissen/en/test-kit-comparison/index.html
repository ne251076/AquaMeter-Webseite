Drop Test, App-Scanned Strips, or Photometer?

Three completely different ways to arrive at the same number. An honest comparison with no purchase pressure — so you can pick the testing method that actually fits your tank.

Why multiple methods even exist

All three methods measure fundamentally the same thing: a chemical color reaction tied to the concentration of a substance in the water. The difference is who or what reads the color — your eye, a smartphone camera, or an optical sensor. That has direct consequences for accuracy, price, and how much time a test takes.

1. Drop test — the classic

e.g. JBL ProAquaTest CombiSet

~$35–45

Add reagent to your water sample drop by drop, compare the color change to a printed color chart. By far the most widely used method in the hobby — and for good reason, as discussions in forums like Flowgrow show: most experienced fishkeepers get along with it just fine.

Pros

  • No smartphone or battery needed
  • Individual tests are cheap to restock
  • Well-established, huge community experience
  • Works for all 13 common parameters

Cons

  • Color comparison is subjective — hard to distinguish similar shades (e.g. nitrite)
  • Even harder to read in tinted water (tannins, alder cones)
  • Reagents have an expiration date

2. App-scanned test strips — the hybrid

e.g. JBL ProScan

~$18–22 kit, ~$16 refill

Dip a test strip in water briefly, place it on an included reference card, photograph it with the free app — the app reads the color and evaluates it automatically. Solves exactly the problem many people have with classic drop tests: subjective color judgment.

Pros

  • Fastest method, under a minute
  • More objective reading than the naked eye
  • App gives you direct recommendations
  • Already covers many important parameters at once

Cons

  • Lighting conditions when photographing affect the result
  • JBL itself recommends double-checking critical nitrite readings with a drop test
  • Refill strips are noticeably more expensive per test than individual drop tests
  • Unusable without a smartphone and the app

3. Photometer — the precision tier

e.g. Hanna Checker HC

~$75 per parameter

An optical sensor measures the color intensity of the reagent reaction and converts it into a precise numerical value — the human eye distinguishes roughly 100 shades of color, a photometer over 1,000. Worth knowing: most single-parameter devices on the market (e.g. from Hanna Instruments) are primarily designed for saltwater/reef tanks; in freshwater, photometers are more of a niche product for enthusiasts.

Pros

  • By far the most precise method
  • Objective, independent of ambient lighting
  • Worthwhile for precise dosing in high-tech planted tanks

Cons

  • Usually one device per parameter — cost adds up fast across multiple readings
  • Overkill for most ordinary freshwater tanks
  • Reagents are still required, only the reading is automated
Voice from the community: Across several Flowgrow discussion threads, the consensus is fairly consistent — for ordinary freshwater fishkeeping, a photometer is more of a "hobby within the hobby" than a necessity. Drop tests are more than enough for most people.

Which one fits you?

Beginner, first tank
Drop test kit

Covers all important readings, teaches you the underlying relationships, cheap to restock.

Multiple tanks, limited time
App-scanned strips

Fastest routine check, especially useful when you're checking several tanks regularly.

Cycling phase / nitrite concern
Drop test

For critical readings like nitrite, the slightly more accurate, well-established method is worth it — or use it as a second opinion alongside a quick test.

High-tech planted tank
Photometer (single parameters)

When exact dosing matters, investing in individual parameter devices makes sense.

Regardless of testing method: keep track over time

Whichever method you choose, the real value comes from tracking results over time. A single reading tells you little; the trend across several weeks shows you whether something's building up before it becomes critical.
